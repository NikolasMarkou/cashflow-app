## Phase 2: Robustness & Realism Validation

### 2.1 Fat-Tailed Distribution Testing

We extended the noise sensitivity analysis to test model robustness against realistic banking data distributions:

| Distribution | WMAPE Mean | WMAPE Std | Pass Rate | vs Gaussian |
|--------------|------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Gaussian (baseline) | 95.92% | 41.96% | 0% | — |
| Student-t (df=3) | 71.23% | 42.38% | 20% | -24.69 pp |
| Student-t (df=5) | 69.97% | 48.91% | 20% | -25.95 pp |
| Student-t (df=10) | 68.00% | 47.19% | 20% | -27.92 pp |
| Laplace | 55.62% | 26.01% | 10% | -40.30 pp |
| Mixture (95%+5% extreme) | 70.40% | 46.59% | 0% | -25.52 pp |

**Key Finding:** Models perform better under heavy-tailed distributions than Gaussian noise at equivalent variance levels. The Laplace distribution shows the best improvement (-40.30 pp vs Gaussian) with the lowest standard deviation (26.01%), suggesting more stable performance under double-exponential noise patterns common in financial data.

### 2.2 Regime Shift Testing

We implemented 9 regime shift scenarios to validate the trend-adjusted projection system:

| Scenario | WMAPE Mean | WMAPE Std | Pass Rate | vs Baseline |
|----------|------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Baseline (no shift) | 26.49% | 10.27% | 20% | — |
| Salary Raise (+£500) | 24.52% | 7.94% | 20% | -1.97 pp |
| Salary Cut (-£800) | 28.61% | 15.17% | 20% | +2.12 pp |
| Multiple Shifts | 27.94% | 12.94% | 20% | +1.45 pp |
| Recent Shift (3 months) | 26.49% | 10.27% | 20% | 0.00 pp |
| Subscription Cancelled | 34.23% | 15.60% | 20% | +7.75 pp |
| Loan Paid Off | 20.76% | 7.94% | **40%** | -5.73 pp |
| Rent Increase (+£200) | 27.56% | 11.99% | 20% | +1.07 pp |
| Lifestyle Change | 30.62% | 18.77% | 20% | +4.13 pp |

**Key Finding:** The system handles positive shifts (salary raise, loan payoff) better than negative shifts or category extinctions. The "Loan Paid Off" scenario achieved the highest pass rate (40%) with the lowest WMAPE (20.76%) and standard deviation (7.94%), indicating the model correctly adapts when large recurring expenses cease. Category extinctions (subscription cancelled) present the greatest challenge (+7.75 pp degradation).

### 2.3 Transfer Tolerance Sweep

We tested transfer detection across different settlement delay configurations:

| Scenario | Optimal Tolerance | F1 Score | Recall @2d |
|----------|-------------------|----------|------------|
| Same-Day Transfers | 0 days | 100% | 100% |
| Domestic Only (0-2d) | 2 days | 100% | 100% |
| Mixed (Domestic + International) | 5 days | 100% | 73.3% |
| International Heavy (3-5d) | 5 days | 100% | 0% |
| High Variance (0-7d) | 7 days | 100% | 36.3% |

*Note: F1 Score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, measuring transfer detection accuracy. A 100% F1 means all actual transfers are detected (recall) without false positives (precision). Recall @2d shows detection performance using the default 2-day tolerance window.*

**Key Finding:** Transfer detection achieves 100% F1 score when tolerance is set appropriately for the transfer mix. The default 2-day tolerance is optimal for domestic-only scenarios but should be increased to 5+ days for portfolios with significant international transfers. Note that Recall @2d drops to 0% for international-heavy portfolios, confirming the need for tolerance tuning based on customer transaction patterns.

### 2.4 Noise Sensitivity Analysis

We ran a comprehensive noise sensitivity analysis across different noise levels:

| Noise Level | WMAPE Mean | WMAPE Std | CI Width | Outliers | Pass Rate | ETS Wins |
|-------------|------------|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|----------|
| Baseline (No Noise) | 27.20% | 23.12% | 646 | 2.7 | 50% | 9/10 |
| Very Low Noise | 22.42% | 10.88% | 430 | 6.8 | 50% | 7/10 |
| Low Noise | 53.41% | 33.80% | 3,456 | 4.8 | 20% | 4/10 |
| Moderate Noise | 80.96% | 40.16% | 6,457 | 1.6 | 10% | 9/10 |
| High Noise | 92.62% | 28.42% | 8,263 | 1.2 | 0% | 5/10 |

**Key Finding:** The system maintains 50% pass rate at baseline and very low noise levels. Performance degrades progressively with increased noise, with confidence interval width scaling proportionally (646 to 8,263). Interestingly, outlier detection counts decrease at higher noise levels (2.7 to 1.2) as the entire signal becomes noisy, making individual outliers less distinguishable.

## Phase 3: Production Alignment

### 3.1 Enhanced Synthetic Data Generator

We created an enhanced synthetic data generator featuring:
- 13 counterparty categories with 100+ unique counterparties
- 5 customer profiles (Young Professional, Family, Retiree, Student, High Earner)
- Realistic category distributions based on banking benchmarks
- Configurable missing data patterns (random and systematic)

### 3.2 Data Quality Contracts

We implemented a DataQualityContract system with:
- **Hard violations:** missing required fields, insufficient history (<24 months), duplicate transactions
- **Soft warnings:** high missing rates, amount outliers, suspicious patterns
- **Preset contracts:** Strict, Lenient, and Default configurations for different use cases

### 3.3 Fallback Behavior

We added graceful degradation to the model selection process:
- Fallback chain: SARIMAX → SARIMA → ETS → Naive (rolling average)
- Automatic model downgrade when primary models fail
- Fallback indicator included in output payload for transparency

### 3.4 Enhanced Confidence Scoring

We replaced the categorical 3-level confidence with a numeric 0-100 score:
- Data quality component (0-25 points)
- History length component (0-25 points)
- Model accuracy component (0-25 points)
- Forecast stability component (0-25 points)

Levels mapped as: High (≥70), Medium (≥40), Low (<40)
